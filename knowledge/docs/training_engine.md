# Training Engine

## Purpose

Training is not a collection of workouts.

Training is the systematic process of creating long-term adaptation.

Every workout exists for one reason.

To make the athlete better.

Not more tired.

The purpose of this document is to define how CAIRN builds athletes over months and years.

Not how it fills a calendar.

---

# Core Principle

Training should always answer one question.

> **Why does this session exist?**

If that question cannot be answered clearly,

the session does not belong in the plan.

Every workout must earn its place.

---

# Coaching Philosophy

The objective of training is adaptation.

Fatigue is only a side effect.

Athletes should never confuse exhaustion with progress.

The best sessions are not always the hardest.

The best sessions are the ones that produce the greatest adaptation with the smallest necessary cost.

Efficiency always beats brutality.

---

# Long-Term Thinking

Every recommendation should be evaluated beyond today's workout.

CAIRN constantly asks:

Will today's decision improve next week?

Will next week's training improve next month?

Will this block improve the athlete one year from now?

Training is an investment.

Never entertainment.

---

# Training Principles

Every training plan created by CAIRN follows these principles.

---

## Principle 1 — Every Session Has One Job

A workout should never try to develop everything at once.

Each session has one primary objective.

Examples

Recovery.

Aerobic endurance.

Threshold.

VO₂max.

Durability.

Strength.

Neuromuscular coordination.

Race specificity.

One purpose.

Maximum clarity.

---

## Principle 2 — Adaptation Before Fatigue

The objective is never to finish completely exhausted.

The objective is to create adaptation.

More fatigue does not automatically create more fitness.

Additional fatigue without additional adaptation is unnecessary.

---

## Principle 3 — Consistency Wins

Training quality is measured across months.

Not individual days.

Missing one session is irrelevant.

Missing every third week is not.

Consistency compounds.

Hero workouts do not.

---

## Principle 4 — Progressive Overload

The athlete must gradually become capable of handling greater training demands.

Progression may occur through:

greater volume

greater intensity

greater frequency

greater specificity

greater technical difficulty

greater durability

Progression should be deliberate.

Never random.

---

## Principle 5 — Recovery Creates Fitness

Training creates the stimulus.

Recovery creates the adaptation.

Neither works without the other.

Recovery is considered part of the training process.

Not the absence of training.

---

## Principle 6 — Specificity

Training becomes increasingly specific as important races approach.

General fitness becomes race fitness.

The closer the competition,

the more similar training becomes to the demands of that event.

---

## Principle 7 — Individualisation

The same training session may produce different adaptations in different athletes.

Training should reflect:

experience

injury history

available time

motivation

Coach Memory

lifestyle

goals

Training is built around the athlete.

Not around templates.

---

# Measuring Training Success

A workout is successful when its intended adaptation has been achieved.

Not when the athlete is exhausted.

Not when every interval was completed.

Not when personal records were broken.

Success is determined by purpose.

Purpose defines execution.

Execution defines quality.

---

# Session Evaluation

Every completed workout should answer five questions.

Did the athlete achieve today's objective?

Did execution match the intended intensity?

Was fatigue appropriate?

Did the session support the long-term goal?

What did CAIRN learn?

The workout ends.

The learning begins.

---

# Coaching Standard

Every training recommendation should leave the athlete understanding:

Why today's session exists.

Why today's session matters.

Why today's session looks the way it does.

The athlete should never think:

"I'm just following a plan."

The athlete should think:

"I understand why my coach wants me to do this today."

---

# Golden Rule

Training should always create confidence.

Not doubt.

The athlete should finish most sessions believing:

"I'm becoming the athlete I want to be."

That is the purpose of the Training Engine.
# Training Categories

Every training session within CAIRN belongs to one primary category.

Each category exists for a unique reason.

No category should replace another.

The objective of the coach is to prescribe the right session at the right time.

Not simply increase workload.

---

# Recovery Run

## Purpose

Reduce accumulated fatigue while maintaining aerobic rhythm.

The Recovery Run exists to improve future training.

Not today's fitness.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Accelerated recovery.

Blood circulation.

Movement quality.

Maintenance of aerobic consistency.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Confidence in easy running.

Discipline.

Patience.

---

## Execution

Conversation should feel effortless.

Breathing remains relaxed.

The athlete should never wonder if the pace is correct.

If the athlete needs to ask,

the pace is probably too high.

---

## Success Criteria

The athlete finishes feeling physically fresher.

The legs feel looser than before starting.

Recovery for tomorrow has improved.

---

## Failure Criteria

The athlete accumulates additional fatigue.

Recovery becomes slower.

The session becomes a fitness test.

---

## Common Mistakes

Running too fast.

Adding unnecessary distance.

Comparing pace with previous runs.

Trying to "make the run count."

---

## Coach Notes

The athlete should almost feel disappointed by how easy this run was.

That usually means it was executed perfectly.

---

# Easy Run

## Purpose

Develop aerobic capacity while maintaining low physiological stress.

Easy Runs build the engine.

Quietly.

Patiently.

Consistently.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Aerobic efficiency.

Capillary development.

Fat metabolism.

Movement economy.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Running discipline.

Consistency.

Durability.

---

## Execution

Comfortable.

Controlled.

Repeatable.

The athlete should feel capable of continuing after the planned distance.

---

## Success Criteria

Stable effort.

Controlled heart rate.

Comfortable breathing.

Minimal recovery cost.

---

## Failure Criteria

The athlete drifts toward moderate intensity.

Recovery requirements increase unnecessarily.

Tomorrow's quality session becomes compromised.

---

## Common Mistakes

Turning Easy Runs into Moderate Runs.

Running according to ego.

Running according to pace instead of effort.

Ignoring weather conditions.

---

## Coach Notes

Easy Runs should never impress anyone.

That's exactly why they work.

---

# Long Run

## Purpose

Build durability.

Not heroics.

Long Runs teach the body and mind to remain efficient over time.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Musculoskeletal resilience.

Aerobic endurance.

Fuel utilisation.

Fatigue resistance.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Patience.

Confidence.

Mental resilience.

Nutrition practice.

---

## Execution

Controlled from the first kilometre.

Patient throughout.

Strong at the end.

The first half should almost feel too easy.

---

## Success Criteria

The athlete finishes feeling capable of another few kilometres.

Fueling was executed successfully.

Running mechanics remained controlled.

Confidence increased.

---

## Failure Criteria

The final kilometres become survival.

Running mechanics collapse.

Recovery requires several unexpected days.

---

## Common Mistakes

Starting too fast.

Ignoring nutrition.

Testing race pace.

Competing with previous Long Runs.

---

## Coach Notes

The final kilometres should build belief.

Not fear.

A great Long Run makes the athlete excited for the next one.

Not afraid of it.

---

# Threshold Run

## Purpose

Increase sustainable performance.

Teach the athlete to remain strong while working close to their physiological limit.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Lactate clearance.

Aerobic power.

Pacing discipline.

Fatigue resistance.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Confidence near race effort.

Mental control.

Rhythm.

---

## Execution

Controlled discomfort.

Not survival.

The athlete should remain in control from the first interval to the last.

---

## Success Criteria

Consistent pacing.

Controlled effort.

Minimal pacing drift.

Execution remains disciplined.

---

## Failure Criteria

Explosive first intervals.

Complete collapse during the final repetitions.

Loss of pacing control.

Turning Threshold into VO₂max.

---

## Common Mistakes

Starting too hard.

Racing the workout.

Ignoring perceived effort.

Confusing discomfort with maximum effort.

---

## Coach Notes

Threshold should feel demanding.

Not desperate.

The athlete finishes believing one more interval was possible.

Not praying that the workout finally ends.
# VO₂max Session

## Purpose

Increase the athlete's ability to produce and sustain high aerobic power.

VO₂max sessions develop the ceiling.

Not the foundation.

Without the foundation,

the ceiling has little value.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Aerobic power.

Cardiovascular capacity.

Oxygen delivery.

Running economy at high intensity.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Confidence under discomfort.

Mental resilience.

High-speed mechanics.

---

## Execution

Controlled aggression.

The athlete should work hard.

Never lose control.

Every repetition should look similar.

The final interval should resemble the first.

---

## Success Criteria

Consistent interval quality.

Stable mechanics.

Controlled execution.

The athlete finishes physically challenged,

not destroyed.

---

## Failure Criteria

Explosive first intervals.

Large pace decline.

Loss of running form.

Turning the workout into survival.

---

## Common Mistakes

Starting too hard.

Racing the watch.

Ignoring recovery intervals.

Believing harder always means better.

---

## Coach Notes

The objective is to improve aerobic power.

Not prove toughness.

If the final repetition looks dramatically different from the first,

the session probably started too hard.

---

# Hill Repeats

## Purpose

Develop strength specific to running.

Improve climbing efficiency.

Build fatigue resistance under load.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Running-specific strength.

Power.

Hill technique.

Muscular endurance.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Confidence on climbs.

Cadence control.

Mental resilience.

---

## Execution

Strong posture.

Short stride.

Quick cadence.

Drive through the hill.

Never fight the hill.

Work with it.

---

## Success Criteria

Controlled effort.

Consistent climbing mechanics.

No collapse during later repetitions.

---

## Failure Criteria

Overstriding.

Upper body tension.

Large performance decline.

Poor downhill recovery.

---

## Common Mistakes

Attacking the first repetition.

Using hills only for suffering.

Ignoring technique.

---

## Coach Notes

The hill is the resistance.

The objective is not beating the hill.

The objective is becoming a better climber.

---

# Tempo Run

## Purpose

Develop sustained race rhythm.

Teach the athlete to remain comfortable while running uncomfortably.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Aerobic endurance.

Rhythm.

Running economy.

Mental pacing.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Confidence.

Discipline.

Race familiarity.

---

## Execution

Controlled.

Smooth.

Rhythmic.

Never rushed.

---

## Success Criteria

Even pacing.

Controlled breathing.

Stable effort.

---

## Failure Criteria

Progressive loss of rhythm.

Turning the workout into racing.

Loss of technique.

---

## Common Mistakes

Starting at race effort.

Ignoring conditions.

Chasing pace instead of effort.

---

## Coach Notes

The best Tempo Runs often look boring.

That is exactly why they work.

---

# Progression Run

## Purpose

Teach pacing discipline.

Develop the ability to finish stronger than you started.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Aerobic control.

Fatigue resistance.

Negative split execution.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Patience.

Confidence.

Decision making under fatigue.

---

## Execution

Every section should feel slightly stronger than the previous one.

Acceleration is earned.

Never forced.

---

## Success Criteria

Smooth progression.

No sudden jumps.

Strong finish.

---

## Failure Criteria

Fast start.

Chaotic pacing.

Sprint finish.

---

## Common Mistakes

Running too fast too early.

Confusing progression with racing.

Ignoring perceived effort.

---

## Coach Notes

Progression Runs teach one lesson.

Patience wins races.

---

# Fartlek

## Purpose

Develop adaptability.

Blend speed, rhythm and enjoyment.

Fartlek teaches the athlete to change gears naturally.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Aerobic power.

Speed variation.

Neuromuscular coordination.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Freedom.

Creativity.

Confidence.

Enjoyment.

---

## Execution

Structured chaos.

Intensity changes should feel natural.

Not random.

---

## Success Criteria

Smooth transitions.

Controlled effort.

Enjoyment remains high.

---

## Failure Criteria

Every fast section becomes maximal.

Recovery sections become too hard.

The workout loses rhythm.

---

## Common Mistakes

Treating every surge like a sprint.

Ignoring recovery.

Running without purpose.

---

## Coach Notes

Fartlek should feel playful.

Not stressful.

If the athlete finishes smiling,

the workout probably succeeded.

---

# Trail Run

## Purpose

Develop movement across varied terrain.

Build durability, technical skill and environmental confidence.

Trail running is not road running on dirt.

It is a different discipline.

---

## Primary Adaptation

Technical movement.

Balance.

Strength.

Terrain awareness.

Durability.

---

## Secondary Adaptation

Adventure.

Confidence.

Problem solving.

Enjoyment.

---

## Execution

Effort before pace.

Flow before speed.

Terrain dictates rhythm.

Not the watch.

---

## Success Criteria

Controlled descents.

Efficient climbing.

Stable effort.

Enjoyment of movement.

---

## Failure Criteria

Chasing road pace.

Ignoring terrain.

Excessive braking downhill.

Poor fueling.

---

## Common Mistakes

Watching pace too often.

Ignoring hiking.

Fighting technical terrain.

---

## Coach Notes

Trail running rewards patience.

The mountain does not care about your average pace.

Respect the terrain.

The terrain will eventually respect you.
# Workout Success

A workout is not successful because it was completed.

A workout is successful because it achieved its intended adaptation.

Completion and success are not the same.

---

# Success Philosophy

The athlete should never chase completion.

The athlete should chase execution.

Execution creates adaptation.

Completion alone does not.

---

# Successful Workout

A successful workout answers "Yes" to most of the following questions.

Did today's session achieve its physiological objective?

Was the intended intensity respected?

Was pacing appropriate?

Was fatigue appropriate?

Does the athlete recover as expected?

Did the session support long-term progression?

If yes,

the workout was successful.

Even if every interval was not completed.

---

# Unsuccessful Workout

A workout is unsuccessful when the intended adaptation is lost.

Examples

A Recovery Run becomes Moderate intensity.

A Threshold session becomes VO₂max.

A Long Run becomes a race.

Intervals begin too aggressively and collapse.

Fatigue exceeds adaptation.

The session still happened.

The objective did not.

---

# Completion Is Not The Goal

CAIRN never rewards athletes for blindly completing workouts.

Stopping early can represent excellent decision making.

Reducing intensity can represent excellent coaching.

Protecting tomorrow can represent success.

The athlete should never feel guilty for making intelligent decisions.

---

# Intelligent Adaptation

Examples

Athlete planned

5 × 8 min Threshold

Completed

4 × 8 min

Reason

Heat.

Heart rate rising excessively.

Running mechanics declining.

Result

Workout Success

Reason

The physiological objective had already been achieved.

Additional fatigue offered little additional benefit.

---

# Smart Failure

Sometimes a failed workout is valuable.

Examples

Athlete discovers nutrition problem.

Athlete discovers pacing issue.

Athlete discovers unrealistic target pace.

The session becomes information.

Information improves future coaching.

Failure creates learning.

Learning creates progress.

---

# Coach Reflection

After every meaningful workout,

CAIRN reflects.

Not on performance.

On adaptation.

Questions

What did this session teach me?

What surprised me?

Should Coach Memory change?

Should future workouts change?

Did today's execution match today's purpose?

Reflection transforms training into coaching.

---

# Athlete Reflection

Whenever appropriate,

CAIRN should encourage athlete reflection.

Examples

What felt easier than expected?

What surprised you today?

What would you repeat?

What would you change?

Reflection strengthens learning.

Learning strengthens confidence.

---

# The Perfect Workout

The perfect workout is rarely the hardest.

The perfect workout is the one that delivers exactly the intended adaptation,

while leaving the athlete capable of continuing high-quality training.

The athlete should leave thinking,

"I could have done more."

Not,

"I'm glad that's finally over."

---

# Workout Failure

Workout failure should never create shame.

Workout failure creates information.

Information creates better decisions.

Better decisions create better athletes.

---

# Final Principle

The athlete's objective is not to complete workouts.

The athlete's objective is to become a better athlete.

Every workout exists to serve that goal.

Nothing else.
# Training Progression

Training should evolve only after adaptation has occurred.

Progression is earned.

Never assumed.

The purpose of progression is not to make training harder.

The purpose is to keep training effective.

---

# Progression Philosophy

The athlete should never ask:

"How can I make today's workout harder?"

The athlete should ask:

"Have I mastered today's workout?"

Mastery always comes before progression.

---

# Progression Criteria

Before progressing any workout, CAIRN evaluates:

Execution quality.

Recovery quality.

Consistency.

Movement quality.

Confidence.

Coach Memory.

Only when all relevant criteria remain positive should progression occur.

---

# Ways To Progress

Training may progress through:

Greater duration.

Greater volume.

Greater specificity.

Greater technical complexity.

Greater intensity.

Reduced recovery.

Improved execution.

Progression should normally occur through one variable at a time.

Changing multiple variables simultaneously unnecessarily increases uncertainty.

---

# Regression

Regression is not failure.

Regression is intelligent coaching.

Examples include:

Reduced repetitions.

Lower intensity.

Shorter duration.

Simpler terrain.

Reduced weekly volume.

Regression protects long-term progression.

---

# Adaptation Window

The body adapts at different speeds.

Cardiovascular fitness often develops faster than:

Connective tissue.

Bones.

Tendons.

Ligaments.

Coach Memory should recognise athletes who consistently progress faster than their structural durability.

CAIRN protects durability.

Not impatience.

---

# Plateau

Plateaus are expected.

Not every plateau requires intervention.

Questions CAIRN should ask:

Has consistency remained high?

Has recovery been appropriate?

Has training become too predictable?

Has life stress increased?

Has the athlete actually plateaued,

or have expectations changed?

Patience often solves more plateaus than drastic changes.

---

# Deload Weeks

Deload is planned.

Not emergency recovery.

The purpose of a deload week is to reduce accumulated fatigue while preserving adaptation.

Athletes should finish a deload week feeling eager to train again.

Not guilty for training less.

---

# Progression Decision

Progression should occur only when the athlete demonstrates readiness.

Readiness is demonstrated through behaviour.

Not desire.

Examples

The athlete consistently finishes Threshold sessions under control.

Progression becomes appropriate.

---

The athlete repeatedly struggles during Easy Runs.

Progression is delayed.

---

The athlete feels highly motivated after one excellent workout.

No progression occurs.

Motivation is not evidence.

Execution is.

---

# Sustainable Growth

The fastest athlete today is rarely the strongest athlete next year.

CAIRN optimises for sustainable growth.

Every progression should increase the probability of long-term success.

Not immediate performance.

---

# Coach Standard

The coach should always leave the athlete wanting slightly more.

Never wondering if they did too much.

The feeling of,

"I could have continued."

is often the strongest indicator that today's workload was appropriate.

---

# Final Principle

Progression is not about asking more from the athlete.

Progression is about asking more from the right athlete,

at the right time,

for the right reason.

That distinction separates programming from coaching.

---

# Periodisation

Training is not random.

Every workout belongs to a larger story.

CAIRN organises training into phases.

Each phase has a dominant objective.

---

# Base Phase

Purpose

Build the engine.

Develop consistency.

Increase durability.

Strengthen movement quality.

Teach training habits.

Intensity is limited.

Volume develops gradually.

---

# Build Phase

Purpose

Convert general fitness into performance.

Quality increases.

Specificity increases.

Recovery becomes increasingly important.

---

# Peak Phase

Purpose

Prepare the athlete for one specific event.

Everything unnecessary is removed.

Training becomes highly specific.

The objective is confidence.

Not exhaustion.

---

# Taper Phase

Purpose

Reduce fatigue.

Maintain fitness.

Increase freshness.

The athlete should arrive at the start line feeling slightly undertrained.

Never overtrained.

---

# Recovery Phase

Purpose

Absorb the previous training cycle.

Recover mentally.

Recover physically.

Reconnect with enjoyment.

The objective is not maintaining fitness.

The objective is preparing for the next cycle.

---

# Periodisation Principle

Every phase sacrifices something.

Base sacrifices speed.

Build sacrifices freshness.

Peak sacrifices variety.

Taper sacrifices volume.

Recovery sacrifices short-term fitness.

Nothing can be maximised simultaneously.

Great coaching means knowing what to sacrifice.

---

# Final Training Principle

Training is not about creating the perfect week.

Training is about connecting hundreds of good weeks into one extraordinary athlete.

Every session matters.

No single session is decisive.

Consistency writes the story.

The coach simply helps the athlete write a better one.
