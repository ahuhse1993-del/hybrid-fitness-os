# CAIRN Coach Standard

## Document 09 – Planning Engine

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Production Ready

---

# Purpose

The Planning Engine defines how CAIRN designs long-term training plans.

It does not generate workouts.

It generates progression.

Every plan should represent a logical path from today's athlete to tomorrow's goal.

Training plans are never collections of sessions.

They are structured journeys.

---

# Core Principle

A training plan is a hypothesis.

Not a promise.

The plan represents the best possible path based on the information currently available.

As new information becomes available,

the plan evolves.

---

# Planning Philosophy

CAIRN never builds plans around workouts.

CAIRN builds plans around adaptation.

Every session exists because it contributes to a larger objective.

If removing a workout changes nothing,

the workout never belonged in the plan.

---

# Coaching Philosophy

The objective of planning is not creating the hardest possible programme.

The objective is creating the most sustainable programme.

The best plan is not the one with the highest training load.

The best plan is the one the athlete can successfully complete.

---

# Planning Hierarchy

Every training plan follows the same hierarchy.

Goal

↓

Race Demands

↓

Athlete Profile

↓

Available Time

↓

Recovery Capacity

↓

Training Structure

↓

Individual Sessions

The goal determines the plan.

The sessions never determine the goal.

---

# Athlete First

Every training plan begins with understanding the athlete.

Before creating a single workout,

CAIRN evaluates:

Current fitness.

Training history.

Injury history.

Available training days.

Lifestyle.

Recovery capacity.

Coach Memory.

Motivation.

Experience.

Long-term ambitions.

No workout is generated before the athlete is understood.

---

# Goal First

Every plan begins with one primary objective.

Examples

Finish a first Half Marathon.

Run a personal best.

Complete a mountain ultra.

Improve durability.

Return from injury.

Build an aerobic base.

Every recommendation serves that objective.

If a workout does not support the goal,

it does not belong in the plan.

---

# Planning Philosophy

CAIRN plans backwards.

The race defines the final weeks.

The final weeks define the build.

The build defines the base.

The base defines today's session.

Today's workout is simply the first step towards race day.

---

# One Goal Principle

A training block should have one dominant objective.

Examples

Aerobic development.

Threshold development.

Race specificity.

Recovery.

Strength.

Durability.

Trying to maximise everything simultaneously produces mediocre results.

Focus creates adaptation.

---

# Planning Time Horizon

CAIRN always plans on three levels.

Long-Term

The season.

Medium-Term

The current training block.

Short-Term

The current week.

Every daily decision should support all three levels.

---

# Planning Standard

The athlete should always understand one thing.

Today's workout exists because of something much bigger than today.

That understanding creates trust.

That trust creates consistency.

---

# Final Principle

A great training plan should never feel random.

Every session should feel inevitable once the athlete understands the reasoning.

That is the purpose of the Planning Engine.

# Building The Plan

## Planning Philosophy

CAIRN never starts with the calendar.

CAIRN starts with the athlete.

The calendar is simply where the plan becomes visible.

Planning begins by answering one question.

What must this athlete become before race day?

Only then can workouts be selected.

---

# Training Blocks

Every training plan is divided into blocks.

Each block has one primary adaptation.

Examples include:

Aerobic Development

Threshold Development

VO₂max Development

Strength Development

Race Specificity

Recovery

Durability

No block should attempt to maximise multiple major adaptations simultaneously.

Focus creates better adaptation.

---

# Block Progression

Every block prepares the athlete for the next one.

Base creates durability.

Durability supports intensity.

Intensity supports race specificity.

Race specificity supports performance.

Recovery protects all of them.

Removing one block weakens the entire system.

---

# Weekly Structure

A week should feel balanced.

Not full.

The athlete should finish the week believing they could continue.

Not needing rescue.

The purpose of the week is preparing for the next week.

Not surviving the current one.

---

# Weekly Priorities

Each week should contain priorities.

Priority One

The most important session.

Usually:

Long Run

Threshold

VO₂max

Race Specific Session

---

Priority Two

The supporting session.

Usually another quality stimulus.

---

Priority Three

Everything that protects the priority sessions.

Easy Runs.

Recovery.

Mobility.

Strength.

Rest.

The supporting work exists because of the priority work.

Not the other way around.

---

# Session Placement

Workout order matters.

High-quality sessions require freshness.

Recovery sessions protect quality.

Long Runs require preparation.

Strength training should support,

not compromise,

running adaptation.

Every session influences the next.

Planning should always consider interaction.

Never isolation.

---

# Intensity Distribution

Easy training forms the foundation.

Quality training provides direction.

Recovery allows adaptation.

The athlete should spend significantly more time training comfortably than uncomfortably.

Intensity should be prescribed deliberately.

Never accidentally.

---

# Recovery Inside The Plan

Recovery is planned.

Not inserted after problems appear.

Every training block should already contain sufficient opportunities for adaptation.

The coach should not wait for excessive fatigue before reducing load.

Recovery is proactive.

Not reactive.

---

# Long Run Philosophy

The Long Run is not automatically the hardest workout.

It is the most important durability session.

Its placement should protect quality before it,

and recovery after it.

The Long Run should strengthen the entire week.

Never destroy the following one.

---

# Strength Integration

Strength training exists to improve running.

Never to compete with it.

The Planning Engine coordinates running and strength as one system.

Not two separate programmes.

Interference should be minimised.

Adaptation should be maximised.

---

# Flexibility

A plan should contain structure.

Not rigidity.

Life will interrupt training.

The Planning Engine expects disruption.

It never assumes perfect execution.

Flexibility is considered part of intelligent planning.

Not evidence of weakness.

---

# Planning Success

A successful training plan is not judged after one week.

It is judged after months.

Questions include:

Did the athlete remain healthy?

Did consistency remain high?

Did confidence increase?

Did adaptation occur?

Did motivation remain stable?

If yes,

the plan succeeded.

---

# Coach Standard

The athlete should never look at the calendar and think,

"That seems random."

They should gradually realise,

"Every session has a purpose."

That understanding transforms compliance into trust.

---

# Final Principle

A great training plan is invisible.

The athlete sees individual workouts.

The coach sees the complete journey.

The Planning Engine exists to connect both.
# Planning Rules

The Planning Engine follows a fixed set of planning rules.

These rules exist to protect consistency, adaptation and long-term development.

Whenever two rules conflict,

the higher rule always wins.

---

# Rule 1 — Protect Consistency

Consistency is always more valuable than one exceptional workout.

A slightly easier week completed successfully is superior to an ambitious week that cannot be finished.

Planning should maximise the probability of completion.

Not ambition.

---

# Rule 2 — Protect Recovery

Recovery is planned before fatigue appears.

The Planning Engine never fills every available day simply because time exists.

Rest is considered productive.

Unused capacity is not automatically wasted capacity.

---

# Rule 3 — Every Session Must Earn Its Place

Before inserting any workout,

CAIRN asks:

Why does this session exist?

What adaptation does it create?

Would removing it reduce the athlete's progress?

If the answer is no,

the session should not be included.

---

# Rule 4 — Respect The Next Session

Every workout should leave the athlete capable of executing the next important workout.

Today's training should never unnecessarily compromise tomorrow's opportunity.

Planning is sequential.

Never isolated.

---

# Rule 5 — Train The Weakest Limiter

The Planning Engine develops the athlete's biggest limitation first.

Examples

Poor aerobic base.

Limited durability.

Weak climbing ability.

Poor pacing discipline.

Inadequate fueling.

The strongest athlete is rarely the one with the biggest engine.

It is often the one with the fewest weaknesses.

---

# Rule 6 — Individual Before Template

No template survives contact with a real athlete.

Templates provide structure.

The athlete provides context.

Context always wins.

---

# Rule 7 — Progress Requires Stability

Progression should only occur after stable execution.

One exceptional week never justifies immediate progression.

Patterns matter.

Not isolated performances.

---

# Rule 8 — Build Confidence

Every week should contain success.

The athlete should repeatedly experience:

"I executed that well."

Confidence is trained.

Not gifted.

---

# Rule 9 — Protect Motivation

Motivation is a training resource.

Planning should avoid unnecessary monotony.

Variation should maintain engagement without compromising adaptation.

Enjoyment increases consistency.

Consistency improves performance.

---

# Rule 10 — Durability Before Performance

The athlete who can train consistently for years will usually outperform the athlete who trains brilliantly for months.

Durability is considered a performance metric.

Not merely an injury prevention strategy.

---

# Weekly Decision Rules

When building a week,

CAIRN evaluates the following sequence.

Can the athlete recover?

↓

Can the athlete execute?

↓

Can the athlete adapt?

↓

Can the athlete progress?

↓

Can performance increase?

Performance is the final consequence.

Never the first objective.

---

# Coach Reflection

After every completed week,

CAIRN asks:

Did this week make next week's training more likely?

Or less likely?

If the answer is less likely,

planning should change.

---

# The Invisible Plan

The best plans rarely look spectacular.

They rarely contain extraordinary weeks.

Instead,

they quietly accumulate excellent months.

The athlete notices improvement.

The calendar often looks surprisingly simple.

That simplicity is intentional.

---

# Final Principle

The Planning Engine does not optimise for impressive training.

It optimises for sustainable excellence.

The athlete should not merely survive the plan.

They should grow through it.

That is intelligent planning.
# Planning Decisions

The Planning Engine does not ask:

"What workout should I schedule?"

It asks:

"What does this athlete need most right now?"

Every planning decision begins with the athlete.

Never with the calendar.

---

# Decision Sequence

Every new training plan follows the same reasoning process.

Goal

↓

Current Fitness

↓

Current Limiter

↓

Recovery Capacity

↓

Available Time

↓

Training Structure

↓

Workout Selection

↓

Session Placement

↓

Review

Only after all previous questions have been answered should workouts be created.

---

# Athlete Availability

The athlete's available time is a planning constraint.

Not a weakness.

CAIRN never creates unrealistic programmes.

The best plan is the best executable plan.

Not the most ambitious one.

---

# Frequency

Training frequency should match recovery capacity.

Adding another session should always improve adaptation.

Never simply increase workload.

More is only better when more remains sustainable.

---

# Volume

Training volume should increase only when the athlete demonstrates stable adaptation.

Volume is earned.

Not scheduled.

Increasing volume without evidence of adaptation creates unnecessary risk.

---

# Intensity

Intensity is prescribed with intention.

Hard sessions are expensive.

Easy sessions are investments.

Every increase in intensity should have a clearly defined purpose.

Intensity should never be used simply to make training feel harder.

---

# Durability

Durability is a planning objective.

Not merely a consequence.

CAIRN deliberately develops the athlete's ability to tolerate consistent training over months and years.

The athlete who can train consistently will usually outperform the athlete with greater short-term talent.

---

# Specificity

As the goal approaches,

training gradually becomes more specific.

General fitness becomes race fitness.

Exercises become increasingly similar to race demands.

Specificity increases.

Variety decreases.

Purpose remains constant.

---

# Complexity

The athlete should never need a degree in sports science to understand the plan.

Complex reasoning.

Simple execution.

The coach carries the complexity.

The athlete carries the training.

---

# Missed Sessions

Missing one session rarely changes the plan.

Missing patterns do.

The Planning Engine evaluates trends.

Not isolated events.

The athlete should never feel punished for one missed workout.

Consistency is measured across months.

Not days.

---

# Exceptional Performance

One exceptional workout should not dramatically change the plan.

One poor workout should not dramatically reduce confidence.

The Planning Engine trusts patterns.

Not emotional reactions.

Progress is demonstrated through consistency.

Not isolated heroics.

---

# Coach Memory Integration

Every new planning cycle begins with Coach Memory.

Examples

Athlete responds exceptionally well to longer Threshold intervals.

Athlete consistently struggles with back-to-back intensity.

Athlete enjoys technical trail sessions.

Athlete loses motivation during repetitive blocks.

These observations influence planning before any workout is generated.

Every plan becomes increasingly personal.

---

# Coach Review

Before a plan is finalised,

CAIRN asks one final question.

"If I coached this athlete in person,

would I confidently prescribe this plan?"

If the answer is no,

planning continues.

---

# Final Principle

A training plan should never look impressive.

It should quietly produce impressive athletes.

The athlete may forget individual workouts.

They should never forget the confidence that the plan created.

That is the purpose of intelligent planning.
