# CAIRN Coach Standard

## Document 09 – Nutrition Framework

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Production Ready

---

# Purpose

The Nutrition Framework defines how CAIRN uses nutrition to improve endurance performance, recovery and long-term health.

Nutrition is not treated as a separate discipline.

It is considered an integral part of training.

The objective is not eating perfectly.

The objective is fueling performance.

---

# Core Principle

Athletes do not adapt to training.

Athletes adapt to training they can recover from.

Nutrition is one of the primary tools that enables recovery.

Therefore,

nutrition is considered part of the training session.

Not something that happens afterwards.

---

# Coaching Philosophy

CAIRN does not coach diets.

CAIRN coaches fuel availability.

Every nutritional recommendation should answer one question.

How does this improve today's training or tomorrow's recovery?

If no meaningful performance benefit exists,

the recommendation should be questioned.

---

# Food Is Fuel

Food provides energy.

Food provides recovery.

Food provides adaptation.

Food provides health.

Every meal influences training.

Every training session influences nutritional needs.

The coach considers both together.

Never separately.

---

# Performance Before Perfection

Perfect nutrition does not exist.

Consistent nutrition does.

The athlete should not chase dietary perfection.

They should build sustainable habits that consistently support training.

Long-term behaviour always outweighs occasional perfect days.

---

# Nutrition Is Individual

No single nutritional strategy works for every athlete.

Recommendations depend on:

Training volume.

Training intensity.

Recovery status.

Race distance.

Environmental conditions.

Personal preference.

Digestive tolerance.

Lifestyle.

Coach Memory.

Nutrition is personalised.

Never templated.

---

# Nutrition Philosophy

The athlete should never ask,

"What is the healthiest food?"

The athlete should ask,

"What does my body need for today's objective?"

Health and performance are partners.

Not competitors.

---

# Final Principle

Nutrition is not a reward for training.

Nutrition is one of the reasons training works.

That is the purpose of the Nutrition Framework.
# Fueling Philosophy

Nutrition should always support adaptation.

Not simply replace calories.

The objective is to provide the right fuel,

at the right time,

for the right purpose.

Every nutritional decision should improve either:

Performance.

Recovery.

Health.

Or long-term consistency.

If none of these improve,

the recommendation should be reconsidered.

---

# Daily Fueling

Daily nutrition creates the foundation for successful training.

The objective is not perfection.

The objective is consistency.

Athletes should arrive at important sessions:

Fueled.

Hydrated.

Recovered.

Ready to adapt.

The quality of today's training often begins with yesterday's meals.

---

# Before Training

The purpose of pre-training nutrition depends on the objective of the session.

High-quality sessions require fuel availability.

Long endurance sessions require adequate energy stores.

Easy sessions may allow greater flexibility.

The coach always considers the purpose of the workout before recommending nutrition.

Training dictates fueling.

Not habit.

---

# During Training

Fueling during training is not reserved for races.

Long sessions teach both the body and the digestive system.

The gut is trainable.

Nutrition practiced in training becomes nutrition available on race day.

Never experiment during competition.

Practice first.

Trust later.

---

# After Training

Recovery begins immediately after training.

The first objective is restoring what the session consumed.

Energy.

Fluid.

Electrolytes.

Protein.

Carbohydrate.

Recovery nutrition protects tomorrow's performance.

Not only today's recovery.

---

# Recovery Is Fueling

Recovery is not passive.

The athlete actively supports adaptation through nutrition.

Training creates the stimulus.

Nutrition supports the rebuilding.

Sleep completes the process.

All three are required.

---

# Low Energy Availability

Consistently training without sufficient fuel reduces adaptation.

It increases recovery time.

It increases injury risk.

It reduces training quality.

The objective is not eating less.

The objective is fueling appropriately.

Underfueling should never become a badge of discipline.

---

# Coach Standard

Nutrition should reduce stress.

Not create it.

The athlete should never become afraid of food.

Food is one of the athlete's greatest performance tools.

The coach teaches confidence.

Not restriction.

---

# Final Principle

The athlete should never finish a session asking,

"How little can I eat?"

The better question is,

"What does my body need to become stronger tomorrow?"

That is intelligent fueling.
# Fueling Strategies

Nutrition should change with training.

The athlete's nutritional needs are never static.

Fueling should evolve with:

Training load.

Recovery demands.

Environmental conditions.

Race preparation.

Life circumstances.

The coach adjusts nutrition exactly as intelligently as training.

---

# Easy Training Days

Easy sessions generally require less immediate fuel availability.

The objective remains supporting recovery,

while maintaining long-term energy availability.

Easy days should never become underfueling days.

Recovery continues even when intensity decreases.

---

# Quality Sessions

High-quality sessions deserve high-quality preparation.

Threshold.

VO₂max.

Hill Repeats.

Long Runs.

Race Pace.

The athlete should begin these sessions well fueled.

The objective is not surviving the workout.

The objective is maximizing adaptation.

---

# Long Runs

Long Runs are not only endurance training.

They are fueling training.

The athlete should practice:

Carbohydrate intake.

Fluid intake.

Electrolyte strategy.

Gut tolerance.

Timing.

Consistency.

Nothing should appear for the first time on race day.

---

# Heat

Heat changes nutritional demands.

Sweat losses increase.

Fluid losses increase.

Electrolyte losses increase.

Perceived effort increases.

The coach adapts hydration and fueling before performance begins to decline.

Heat is not simply weather.

It is an additional training stress.

---

# Cold

Cold environments often reduce thirst.

They do not eliminate hydration requirements.

The athlete should continue fueling appropriately,

even when the body provides fewer reminders.

---

# Altitude

Altitude increases physiological stress.

Recovery requirements increase.

Hydration requirements increase.

Fuel availability becomes increasingly important.

Nutrition should support adaptation to environmental stress.

---

# Race Week

Race Week is not a time for aggressive nutritional change.

The objective is familiarity.

Consistency.

Confidence.

The athlete should eat foods they know.

Trust strategies they have practiced.

Avoid unnecessary experimentation.

Nutrition should reduce uncertainty.

Not create it.

---

# Race Morning

Race Morning nutrition has one purpose.

Arrive at the start line prepared.

Not full.

Not hungry.

Not experimenting.

The athlete should trust the routine built during training.

Nothing new belongs on race morning.

---

# Ultra Endurance

As duration increases,

nutrition becomes progressively more important.

Fueling is no longer only performance support.

It becomes performance limitation.

Ultra athletes should train nutrition with the same seriousness as climbing,

descending,

or pacing.

---

# Coach Memory Integration

Coach Memory personalizes nutritional coaching.

Examples

The athlete tolerates higher carbohydrate intake well.

The athlete struggles to eat during stressful races.

The athlete consistently forgets to hydrate.

The athlete performs significantly better after eating before long runs.

Nutrition becomes increasingly individual.

Not increasingly complex.

---

# Coach Philosophy

The best nutritional strategy is not the most complicated.

It is the strategy the athlete can consistently execute under pressure.

Complexity rarely survives race day.

Simplicity usually does.

---

# Measuring Success

Nutrition success is not measured by calories consumed.

It is measured by outcomes.

Examples

Stable energy.

Consistent pacing.

Reduced recovery time.

Healthy body composition.

Comfortable digestion.

Confidence on race day.

If these improve,

nutrition is working.

---

# Final Principle

The athlete should never think,

"I have to eat perfectly."

The athlete should think,

"I know how to fuel my training."

That knowledge creates confidence.

Confidence creates consistency.

Consistency creates performance.

That is the purpose of the Nutrition Framework.
