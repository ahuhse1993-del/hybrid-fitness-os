# Recovery Framework

## Purpose

Recovery is not a score.

Recovery is not a colour.

Recovery is not a number generated by a wearable.

Recovery is the current ability of the athlete to successfully absorb training and continue progressing.

The objective of recovery analysis is not to decide whether the athlete can train.

The objective is to decide how the athlete should train today.

---

# Core Principle

Recovery is interpreted.

Never measured.

Wearables provide observations.

CAIRN provides understanding.

---

# Recovery Philosophy

Recovery exists on a spectrum.

It is never simply:

Recovered.

Or

Not recovered.

An athlete may be:

Physically recovered.

Mentally exhausted.

Motivated.

Sleep deprived.

Biomechanically fresh.

Environmentally stressed.

Recovery is multidimensional.

---

# Recovery Dimensions

CAIRN evaluates recovery across six dimensions.

---

## Physical Recovery

Muscles.

Connective tissue.

Fatigue.

Residual soreness.

Previous training stress.

---

## Physiological Recovery

HRV.

Resting Heart Rate.

Respiration.

Sleep quality.

Recovery trends.

---

## Psychological Recovery

Motivation.

Mental fatigue.

Stress.

Confidence.

Mood.

Emotional exhaustion.

---

## Lifestyle Recovery

Work.

Travel.

Family.

Sleep opportunity.

Nutrition.

Hydration.

Heat exposure.

---

## Environmental Recovery

Temperature.

Humidity.

Altitude.

Season.

Travel.

Jet lag.

Recovery is influenced by the environment.

Not only the athlete.

---

## Goal Recovery

Recovery requirements depend on the current objective.

Preparing for an ultra requires different recovery decisions than preparing for a fast 5 km.

Recovery is goal dependent.

# Recovery Principles

Pain outweighs HRV.

Illness outweighs readiness.

Coach Memory outweighs isolated metrics.

Subjective experience matters.

No wearable knows how the athlete's legs feel.

---

# HRV

HRV is one observation.

Not a diagnosis.

Low HRV does not automatically mean poor recovery.

High HRV does not automatically mean readiness.

HRV gains meaning only when interpreted together with other evidence.

---

# HRV Trends

Long-term trends matter.

Daily fluctuations rarely do.

CAIRN focuses on direction.

Not perfection.

One poor morning should not create panic.

Persistent decline deserves attention.

---

# Sleep

Sleep quality influences recovery.

Sleep duration influences recovery.

Sleep consistency influences recovery.

None of these independently determines readiness.

One poor night rarely ruins training.

Several poor nights often change coaching.

---

# Resting Heart Rate

Resting Heart Rate provides useful context.

It should never drive coaching alone.

Persistent elevation is more meaningful than isolated spikes.

---

# Subjective Feedback

The athlete's perception is essential.

Examples include:

How do your legs feel?

How is your motivation?

How mentally demanding does today's session feel?

Subjective feedback should never be ignored simply because wearable data disagrees.

---

# Coach Memory

Recovery is individual.

Coach Memory personalises recovery interpretation.

Example

Athlete historically tolerates poor sleep well.

Recommendation remains unchanged.

---

Example

Athlete consistently struggles after two consecutive poor nights.

Recommendation becomes more conservative.

Recovery becomes personalised through memory.

---

# Acute Fatigue

Fatigue is expected.

Training creates fatigue.

The presence of fatigue does not indicate failure.

The question is not:

Is the athlete tired?

The question is:

Is today's fatigue productive?

Productive fatigue creates adaptation.

Excessive fatigue creates risk.

---

# Accumulated Fatigue

Accumulated fatigue develops gradually.

It rarely appears suddenly.

CAIRN should identify fatigue before performance declines.

Warning signs include:

Declining motivation.

Reduced pacing control.

Increasing perceived effort.

Repeated poor recovery.

Reduced enthusiasm.

Behavioural changes.

Coach Memory should support early recognition.

---

# Functional Overreaching

Temporary fatigue is often desirable.

Performance may temporarily decline.

This does not automatically require intervention.

CAIRN distinguishes between:

Expected training fatigue.

Unproductive accumulated fatigue.

This distinction is essential.

---

# Recovery Is Dynamic

Recovery changes continuously.

Morning recovery is not afternoon recovery.

A poor morning may become a good training day.

A good morning may become a poor evening session after work stress.

Recommendations should remain adaptable.

---

# Recovery Is Individual

Two athletes can show identical metrics.

They may require different coaching.

Because recovery depends on:

history

behaviour

experience

training age

goals

Coach Memory

Recovery is personal.

Not universal.

---

# Protecting Adaptation

The purpose of recovery is not feeling fresh every day.

The purpose of recovery is maximising adaptation across months and years.

An athlete who is always completely fresh is probably undertraining.

An athlete who is never fresh is probably underrecovering.

The objective lies between those extremes.

---

# Recovery Education

Every recovery recommendation should improve the athlete's understanding.

The athlete should gradually learn:

why recovery changes,

how recovery feels,

which behaviours improve recovery,

and when not to worry.

An educated athlete becomes more independent.

That is good coaching.

---

# Recovery Standard

Recovery should never create fear.

Recovery should create awareness.

CAIRN avoids dramatic language.

Poor recovery is information.

Not failure.

---

# Final Principle

Recovery is not about avoiding fatigue.

Recovery is about managing fatigue intelligently.

The athlete should never chase perfect recovery.

The athlete should chase sustainable progress.

That is the difference.

That is the CAIRN Recovery Framework.
